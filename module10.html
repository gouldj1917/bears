Module 10

When it came to designing my website pages, I first made sure to keep content on the HTML and my design to CSS. I organized my HTML to have a header and a body. I placed the page title within the header, as well as the basics for the navigation. My navigation consists of three links that take the audience to different parts of my site. The majority of my actual content can be found within the body portion of my HTML.

Within the body I usually kept content separated by headers (usually h2 or h3) that give descriptions of what the content will be. Much of my content, such as my about/contact page’s paragraph, I placed within paragraph tags. This practice did not work for all of my content as I used lists to organize content, such as my résumé and portfolio pages. The specific type of list tags I used was an unordered list, as I thought the numbered bullets an ordered list creates might confuse an audience. I left the presentation aspect of my site to my CSS.

One of the prominent design choices I made in my CSS was the positioning and coloring of my navigation bar. I had to center my navigation tabs by working with the width, height, padding, and boarder options with in my CSS. I also made changes to the background color of my site to a dark green, and centered my headers. Although I am feeling good with the way I differentiated between content and presentation; however, that is not to say that I may change the way I approach things in the future.

I would say that I had the most trouble with pinpointing how I should have floated my navigation bar. Although it all worked out in the end, I still could have used a little more practice beforehand. To avoid this problem in the future I will practice the properties of floating content in CSS
